# DRAFT — LinkedIn Article

**Title: What the 2026 agreements mean for the unions' next decade**

In 2023, performers' unions fought for language. In 2026, they got detail: Article 39 of the new SAG-AFTRA TV/Theatrical Agreement is the most specific set of rules the industry has ever had for digital replicas — consent, compensation, security, transfers, training data, strike protection, clause by clause.

The next decade's question is different in kind, and it will define whether 2026 was a victory or a press release: **who checks, and with what evidence?**

(A note on standpoint: we build custody and consent infrastructure, so we have a stake in one answer. This piece is analysis, not a union position — neither SAG-AFTRA nor Equity has endorsed any platform, ours included.)

**The pattern in every clause: an evidence gap.**

Read Article 39 as an auditor rather than a lawyer and the same shape repeats. 39.B requires informed, specific, separate consent — evidenced by what record, retrievable by whom, three years later? 39.C makes replica use compensable per use — counted by what meter? 39.E expects biometric data isolated with restricted access — demonstrated by whose access logs? 39.G restricts replica use during a strike — enforced by which control, and how would the union know it engaged? 39.H demands "commercially reasonable" security — a phrase that gets defined in court, after the leak, unless it's defined first by evidence. 39.I protects transfers — visible to whom when the asset changes hands in a studio merger?

Every clause is a right; almost none arrives with its own enforcement machinery. Contract enforcement by grievance assumes the union eventually *finds out*. In a world where a replica can be generated, used, and deleted between residuals statements, finding out is the hard part. The gap between negotiated terms and observable compliance is where the next decade's disputes will live.

**Oversight without custody.**

The instinctive fix — give the union access to the data — is wrong, and unions know it best of all. A union holding scan files becomes a custodian with liability; a union with production-system logins becomes an IT-security problem; both compromise the arms-length position a union needs across thousands of employers.

The architecture that fits is a **watcher role**: read-only, affiliation-scoped visibility into the *evidence layer* — consent records, use counts, strike-lock engagements, deletion attestations, transfer events for covered members — with no keys to the data plane and no ability to touch the files themselves. Oversight without custody. The same shape works for the other quiet arrival in this space: insurers, whose E&O and completion-bond underwriting is beginning to price continuous compliance evidence. When the union's compliance view and the insurer's underwriting view read from the same records, "commercially reasonable" stops being rhetoric and starts being a grade.

**The training-data question is the next war.**

39.L allows producers to license performances for AI training, with notice to the union — and no performer revenue share required by default. It is the largest untapped value pool in the agreement, and as written it clears at whatever the least-informed party accepts.

Expect this to dominate the next cycle. The structural counterweights are already visible: opt-in registries where training consent is off until the performer enables it; talent-set pricing rather than producer-set offers; union notice generated automatically as a by-product of the transaction instead of a letter someone remembers to send. Whether those become contract terms is a negotiation question. That they are *possible* changes the negotiation — "it can't be tracked" is no longer an available argument.

**Two unions, one architecture.**

The UK is not covered by Article 39. Equity members live under Equity agreements, UK GDPR — where biometric data is special-category, carrying obligations Article 39 doesn't dream of — and, increasingly, the EU AI Act's Article 50 provenance rules. The rights frameworks genuinely differ.

The evidencing machinery shouldn't. Consent records, use meters, access logs, deletion attestations are regime-agnostic primitives; SAG-AFTRA, Equity, GDPR Article 9, BIPA, and the AI Act can each be expressed as a compliance regime *reading* the same event stream. For unions, that has a practical consequence: a member scanned on a US production and a UK co-production shouldn't need two philosophies of enforcement — and jurisdictional arbitrage ("we'll scan where nobody's checking") only closes if the evidence layer is portable.

**Dear negotiating committees: ask for the ledger.**

If we could put one idea in front of every committee drafting for the next cycle, it's this. The 2023–2026 arc won the *rights*; the instrument that makes rights real is the *record*. Make evidence itself a contract term:

- Consent as signed, versioned events — not signatures in deal memos.
- Replica use as metered counts the payroll side can't round down.
- Deletion as attestation within a deadline — not silence.
- Transfers with union-visible records as a condition, not a courtesy.
- Strike protection as a control that demonstrably engaged — with the engagement on the record.

None of that requires endorsing any vendor, ours included; it requires specifying *outcomes* — that the record exist, be tamper-evident, and be producible on request — and letting employers choose machinery that meets the spec. Auditability as a negotiated term, the way finance settled it decades ago.

The unions spent three years winning the language. The decade ahead is about making the language observable. The side with the records tends to win the grievance — and for the first time, the records are buildable.
