# DRAFT — LinkedIn Article

**Title: SAG's AI rules are now live. Here's what productions actually have to evidence.**

On 1 July 2026, the new SAG-AFTRA TV/Theatrical Agreement came into effect — and with it, the most detailed rules the industry has ever had for digital replicas of performers. Article 39 is no longer a negotiating position. It's the operating environment for every covered production through 2030.

Having spent the last year building consent and custody infrastructure for exactly this problem, here's our plain-English read of what productions are now expected to *evidence* — not just comply with, but prove. (One caveat up front: this covers SAG-AFTRA jurisdiction. UK productions live under Equity agreements and UK GDPR, where biometric data is special-category data — a different regime with rhyming obligations.)

**The headline change: consent became a record, not a signature.**

Under 39.B, consent to create and use a digital replica must be informed and specific — a description of the intended use, separate from the employment agreement. California law now goes further: replica terms signed without informed consent can be void. A signature block buried in a deal memo doesn't clear that bar. What does: a consent record that names the use type, scope, and production, timestamped, and retrievable three years later when someone asks.

**Clause by clause, here's what "evidence" looks like:**

**39.A — replicas without scans.** Producers can now create a replica from footage captured on set, no scan session required. This matters because it kills the comfortable assumption that controlling the scan file controls the replica. Consent and usage records are the durable control point — not custody of a mesh. (We build a vault, so believe us when we say this was uncomfortable to accept.)

**39.C — replica use carries minimums and residuals.** Every independently-created digital replica use is a compensable event. That only works operationally if uses are *counted*. If your pipeline can generate output with a performer's likeness, something needs to meter it — per generation, per render — or payroll is guessing.

**39.D — dubbing consent went per-language.** Using a performer's voice or likeness for dubs now needs consent per language and territory. Most productions have no system that stores consent at that granularity. Spreadsheets will be tried. Spreadsheets will fail audits.

**39.E — biometric data isolation.** Scan data is expected to be held separately, with restricted access. The cleanest way to evidence isolation is for the data not to sit in the production's own storage at all — held instead by a party whose access logs you can point to.

**39.G — strike protection.** During a strike, replica use is restricted. The question nobody asked before: what does a strike *technically* mean for your pipeline? If the answer is "we'd send an email asking teams to stop," that's not a control. A control is a lock that blocks the use and records that it blocked it.

**39.H — replica security.** Producers must make "commercially reasonable efforts" to secure replicas. "Commercially reasonable" is a phrase that gets defined in court, after the leak — unless you define it first with evidence: access controls, custody logs, tamper detection, and a record of who touched what, when.

**39.I — transfers.** When replicas change hands — asset sales, studio mergers — transfers need protection and, in some cases, union visibility. A transfer that lives in an email chain is a dispute waiting for a docket number.

**39.L — AI training.** Producers may license performances for AI training with notice to the union. Note what's missing: performer revenue share isn't required by default. Expect this to be the most contested clause of the next cycle — and expect performers and their reps to start asking, deal by deal, what your training-data position is.

**The uncomfortable summary**

Almost every obligation above reduces to the same question: *can you produce the record?* Consent records, use counts, access logs, security attestations, deletion proof. Today, on most productions, those records live in PDFs, inboxes, and the memories of people who've since moved on to other shows.

The productions that will find the next four years easy are the ones that treat Article 39 the way finance treats an audit: evidence generated continuously, by systems, as a by-product of normal work — not reconstructed in a panic when a claim lands.

**A checklist worth running this week:**

1. Can you list every performer scanned on your current production, and produce their replica consent — specific to use type — in under an hour?
2. Is consent to the replica separate from the employment signature?
3. Who, by name, can access your scan data right now? Is there a log?
4. If a licence or agreement ends, what deletes the data — a person remembering, or a system? Can you prove deletion happened?
5. If a strike were declared tomorrow, what would technically stop replica use on your production?

If any answer is "we'd have to check," that's the gap.
