# DRAFT — LinkedIn Article

**Title: Your likeness as an earning asset: buyouts, meters, and the new deal structures**

For a century, a performer's likeness earned money one way: show up, perform, get paid. The scan era added a second: sign a buyout, hand over the file, hope the paragraph about "in perpetuity, all media" never matters.

Generative AI broke that model — quietly, and in the performer's favour, if the deal structures catch up. Here's the landscape as we see it from inside the infrastructure.

**The buyout's failure mode.**

A buyout prices a likeness like a prop: one fee, one transfer, done. That made rough sense when reuse meant an expensive VFX shot. It makes no sense when one licensed asset can drive ten thousand generations — game builds, dubs, marketing variants, synthetic performances — at near-zero marginal cost. Under a buyout, every one of those generations is value the performer already sold, unpriced, in clause 47.

The 2026 SAG-AFTRA agreement pushed back: 39.C makes independently-created digital replica use a compensable event with minimums and residuals. But a right to per-use compensation is only worth what your counting infrastructure can evidence. If nobody meters, payroll is guessing.

**Deal structure one: the licence menu.**

The first fix is boring and powerful: stop negotiating bespoke contracts and pick from a menu. Six standard licence types — commercial, film double, game character, AI avatar, training data, monitoring reference — each with defined scope, duration, and a public fee band (film double £50–300k; game character £100–500k; AI avatar priced per campaign).

Menus beat bespoke negotiation for the same reason rate cards beat haggling: faster deals, comparable data, and no surprise categories. "Training data" is a line item you must explicitly touch — never a rider buried in the definitions section.

**Deal structure two: the meter.**

Metered licensing is the real shift. A licence issues a royalty key; every generation or render reports against it; the performer's split is computed server-side and accrues live — 80% talent, 10% agency, 10% platform is the standard split on metered deals.

For talent, that turns a likeness from a thing you sold once into a thing that earns while you sleep. For reps, "commission" becomes recurring flow with a live dashboard instead of a one-time percentage. For licensees it's often *cheaper*: you pay for a thousand generations, not a lifetime buyout you'll never fully use. Nobody needs to price the unknowable up front — the meter prices it as it happens.

**Deal structure three: training data, priced and consented.**

Article 39.L lets producers license performances for AI training with notice to the union — and requires no performer revenue share by default. That's the largest untapped pool in the agreement, and right now it clears at whatever the least-informed party accepts.

The counterweight is an opt-in registry with talent-set pricing: consent that is structurally off until the performer switches it on, licences in the £100k–£1M+ band for meaningful catalogues, automatic union notice as a by-product. From exposure to product — but only for those who opt in, at their price.

**Deal structure four: the ladder.**

Fee bands quoted above are working-actor-and-up numbers. For emerging talent we've been running a micro-licence experiment: £500–£2,000, auto-approved, non-exclusive, 12-month terms. Volume economics for licensees, and a career ladder for performers — early credits generate data, data supports higher bands, thresholds promote you into the standard tiers. And because below-market offers are the persistent risk at the entry level, fee guidance is benchmarked against real approved deals (minimum three comparables, no names leaked) so a lowball gets flagged before signature, not lamented after.

**What a rep should ask for in every deal from now on.**

1. **Which of the six licence types is this?** If the answer doesn't map to one, ask why the scope is bespoke.
2. **Is replica consent a separate signature from the employment agreement?** Under 39.B it should be — and in California, uninformed replica terms can be void.
3. **Per-use or buyout — and if per-use, what meters it?** A residuals clause without counting infrastructure is a promise, not a term.
4. **What's the training-data position?** Silence is not neutrality; make it an explicit yes-at-a-price or no.
5. **What happens at expiry?** Access should end mechanically, and deletion should come back as an attestation — in writing, on a ledger — not a shrug.

**The quiet reframe.**

None of this works as clauses alone. It works when consent is an event stream, use is metered, splits are computed where nobody can fudge them, and expiry produces evidence. That's infrastructure, not paperwork — and it's the difference between a likeness as a liability someone else warehouses and a likeness as an asset that reports its own earnings.

The scan is the asset. The person owns it. The deals are finally starting to reflect that.
